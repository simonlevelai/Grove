# Maps of Content (MOCs)

Maps of Content (MOCs) are a structural pattern for organising linked notes, popularised by Nick Milo through his Linking Your Thinking framework. A MOC is a note whose primary purpose is to curate links to other notes around a specific theme.

## The Problem MOCs Solve

In a flat, link-based note system like a Zettelkasten, navigation becomes difficult as the collection grows. With hundreds or thousands of notes, finding your way to a specific idea requires either remembering its title or searching. MOCs provide intermediate waypoints — a note titled "Cognitive Science MOC" might link to notes on memory, attention, decision-making, and learning, giving you a starting point for exploration.

## How MOCs Work

A MOC is simply a note that contains an organised list of links. It might group notes by subtopic, by chronology, or by argument structure. The key distinction from a folder is that MOCs are non-exclusive: a single note can appear in multiple MOCs without being "moved" anywhere.

MOCs can also link to other MOCs, creating a layered navigation structure. A "Psychology MOC" might link to "Cognitive Science MOC", "Behavioural Economics MOC", and "Clinical Psychology MOC", each of which links to its own cluster of atomic notes.

## MOCs vs Folders

Folders enforce a single hierarchy. A note lives in one folder. MOCs are additive: they create organisation without constraining it. A note about "confirmation bias" can appear in a Cognitive Science MOC, a Critical Thinking MOC, and a Decision-Making MOC simultaneously.

## When to Create a MOC

Milo suggests creating a MOC when you notice a cluster of notes forming around a theme — typically after accumulating 5-10 notes on a topic. Creating MOCs too early imposes premature structure; creating them too late means navigating a disorganised mass.
