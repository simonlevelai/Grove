# Evergreen Notes

Evergreen notes are a writing practice developed by Andy Matuschak, a researcher and software engineer formerly at Apple and Khan Academy. The core idea is that notes should be written and maintained as lasting knowledge assets, not disposable records.

## Properties of Evergreen Notes

Matuschak identifies several properties that distinguish evergreen notes from conventional note-taking:

**Atomic.** Each note captures a single concept or claim. This mirrors the Zettelkasten principle of atomicity and makes notes composable — they can be combined and recombined in different contexts.

**Concept-oriented.** Notes are titled as declarative statements rather than topics. "Spaced repetition improves long-term retention" is more useful than "Spaced repetition" because the title itself carries meaning.

**Densely linked.** Evergreen notes form a web through explicit links. Matuschak argues that the links between notes are often more valuable than the notes themselves, because they reveal the structure of your thinking.

**Written in your own voice.** Paraphrasing and synthesis force understanding. Direct quotation is acceptable as supporting evidence but should not constitute the body of a note.

## Evergreen vs Transient Notes

Not all notes need to be evergreen. Meeting minutes, daily journals, and fleeting observations serve immediate purposes and naturally decay in relevance. The distinction matters because it guides where to invest writing effort. Transient notes are captured quickly; evergreen notes are crafted carefully.

## Relationship to Other Systems

Evergreen notes draw heavily on Luhmann's Zettelkasten. The key addition is the emphasis on declarative titles and the explicit acknowledgement that most notes people write are transient. Matuschak's framing helps practitioners decide which ideas deserve the deeper investment of evergreen treatment.
