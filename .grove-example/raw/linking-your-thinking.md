# Linking Your Thinking (LYT)

Linking Your Thinking (LYT) is a personal knowledge management framework developed by Nick Milo. It builds on Zettelkasten principles but adapts them for modern bidirectional linking tools like Obsidian, Logseq, and Roam Research.

## Core Components

**Home note.** A single note that serves as the top-level entry point to your knowledge base. The Home note links to your most important MOCs and active areas of thinking. It answers the question: "Where do I start?"

**Maps of Content (MOCs).** Higher-order notes that curate links around a theme. MOCs are the primary navigational structure in LYT, replacing both folders and tags as the main organisational mechanism.

**Atomic notes.** Individual notes containing a single idea, following the Zettelkasten principle of atomicity. These form the base layer of the system.

## The Fluid Hierarchy

Milo describes LYT's organisation as a "fluid hierarchy" — structure that can emerge, evolve, and dissolve as your thinking changes. Unlike folders, which impose permanent rigid categories, MOCs can be created, merged, split, and retired as needed.

The three levels of the hierarchy are:

1. **Home note** — your entry point
2. **MOCs** — thematic navigation hubs
3. **Atomic notes** — individual ideas

This hierarchy is suggestive, not prescriptive. Notes are not "contained" in MOCs the way files are contained in folders. A note exists independently and can be referenced from any number of MOCs.

## Relationship to Zettelkasten

LYT shares the Zettelkasten's commitment to atomic notes and linking but adds the explicit navigational layer of MOCs and the Home note. Where Luhmann relied on his sequential numbering system for navigation, LYT uses MOCs to provide human-readable entry points into clusters of ideas.
