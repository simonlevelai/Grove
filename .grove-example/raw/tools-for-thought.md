# Tools for Thought

Tools for thought are software applications designed to augment human cognitive abilities. The term originates with Douglas Engelbart's 1962 paper "Augmenting Human Intellect" and was further developed by Howard Rheingold in his 2000 book of the same name.

## The Modern Generation

A wave of tools launched in the late 2010s and early 2020s revived interest in the concept:

**Roam Research** (2019) introduced bidirectional linking and block-level references to a broad audience. Every mention of a page automatically creates a backlink, making connections visible without manual effort.

**Obsidian** (2020) takes a local-first approach, storing notes as plain markdown files on the user's filesystem. Its plugin ecosystem and graph view have made it the most popular tool in the personal knowledge management community.

**Logseq** (2020) combines outliner-based editing with bidirectional linking, appealing to users who think in bullet points and nested structures.

**Notion** (2016, but PKM adoption later) offers databases, templates, and flexible page structures that support a wide range of organisational approaches.

## Shared Features

Despite their differences, modern tools for thought share several features that support networked thinking:

- **Bidirectional links** make connections explicit and navigable in both directions
- **Graph visualisation** reveals the structure of connections between notes
- **Block references** allow linking to specific paragraphs rather than entire pages
- **Local-first or plain text storage** gives users ownership of their data

## Limitations

Tools are enablers, not solutions. A sophisticated linking tool does not automatically produce good thinking. The methodologies — Zettelkasten, evergreen notes, PARA, MOCs — provide the intellectual framework. The tools provide the medium.
