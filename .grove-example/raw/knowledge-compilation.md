# Knowledge Compilation

Knowledge compilation refers to the process of transforming a collection of raw sources — articles, papers, notes, highlights — into a coherent, structured body of knowledge. The term draws an analogy to software compilation: raw source code (scattered notes) is compiled into an executable programme (a navigable, interlinked wiki).

## The Manual Process

In traditional personal knowledge management, compilation is manual. A practitioner reads sources, writes atomic notes, links them together, creates maps of content, and periodically refactors the structure. This process is intellectually rewarding but time-consuming. Many knowledge workers accumulate vast collections of highlights and clippings that never get processed.

## The Bottleneck

The bottleneck in most second brain workflows is the step between capture and synthesis. Tiago Forte's Progressive Summarisation addresses this by deferring processing, but the final synthesis step — turning distilled notes into original articles or structured knowledge — still requires sustained human effort.

This is where automated approaches become interesting. Large language models can read multiple sources simultaneously, identify common themes and contradictions, and produce structured output with citations. The key challenge is maintaining provenance — ensuring that compiled knowledge can be traced back to its sources.

## Principles of Good Compilation

Whether manual or automated, good knowledge compilation should:

1. **Preserve provenance.** Every claim should be traceable to its source.
2. **Surface contradictions.** Conflicting sources should be flagged, not silently resolved.
3. **Maintain atomic structure.** Compiled articles should follow the one-idea-per-article principle.
4. **Enable navigation.** The output should be interlinked and browsable, not monolithic.
5. **Support human override.** The author should be able to annotate, pin, and correct compiled output.

## The Future

LLM-assisted knowledge compilation does not replace human thinking — it accelerates the mechanical parts of the process while leaving judgement, curation, and creative synthesis to the human. The goal is to spend less time copying and formatting, and more time understanding and creating.
