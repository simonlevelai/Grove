# Digital Gardens

A digital garden is an approach to publishing and sharing knowledge on the web. Unlike a blog, which presents posts in reverse chronological order as finished products, a digital garden presents interconnected notes in various stages of development.

## Origins

The term "digital garden" draws on a long history of hypertext thinking, from Vannevar Bush's 1945 "As We May Think" essay to Ted Nelson's concept of the Docuverse. The modern usage was popularised by writers like Mike Caulfield, Maggie Appleton, and Joel Hooks in the late 2010s.

## Key Characteristics

**Growth stages.** Notes are explicitly marked with their maturity: seedlings (rough ideas), budding (developing thoughts), and evergreen (well-developed, stable content). This signals to readers that ideas are works in progress, not finished proclamations.

**Topological navigation.** Readers navigate through links between ideas rather than scrolling a chronological timeline. This mirrors how knowledge is actually structured — as a web of relationships, not a sequence of events.

**Continuous revision.** Garden notes are updated, expanded, and sometimes pruned over time. A note written in 2020 might be substantially different by 2024. This contrasts with blog posts, which are typically published once and rarely revised.

**Imperfection as a feature.** Digital gardens embrace showing work in progress. This lowers the barrier to publishing and encourages learning in public, where the process of thinking is visible alongside the results.

## Relationship to Personal Knowledge Management

Digital gardens are the public-facing expression of practices like Zettelkasten and evergreen notes. Where those systems focus on private knowledge development, digital gardens extend the same principles to shared knowledge, inviting others into the thinking process.
