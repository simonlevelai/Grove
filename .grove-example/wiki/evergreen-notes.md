# Evergreen Notes

Evergreen notes are a writing practice developed by Andy Matuschak, a researcher and software engineer formerly at Apple and Khan Academy. The core idea is that notes should be written and maintained as lasting knowledge assets, not disposable records [source: evergreen-notes.md].

## Properties

Matuschak identifies several properties that distinguish evergreen notes from conventional note-taking:

**Atomic.** Each note captures a single concept or claim, following the [[atomic-notes-principle]]. This makes notes composable — they can be combined and recombined in different contexts [source: evergreen-notes.md]. The right level of atomicity depends on the domain: in academic research, an atomic note might capture a single finding; in project management, a single decision [source: atomic-notes-principle.md].

**Concept-oriented.** Notes are titled as declarative statements rather than topics. "Spaced repetition improves long-term retention" is more useful than "Spaced repetition" because the title itself carries meaning [source: evergreen-notes.md].

**Densely linked.** Evergreen notes form a web through explicit links. Matuschak argues that the links between notes are often more valuable than the notes themselves, because they reveal the structure of your thinking [source: evergreen-notes.md].

**Written in your own voice.** Paraphrasing and synthesis force understanding. Direct quotation is acceptable as supporting evidence but should not constitute the body of a note [source: evergreen-notes.md].

## Evergreen vs Transient

Not all notes need to be evergreen. Meeting minutes, daily journals, and fleeting observations serve immediate purposes and naturally decay in relevance. The distinction matters because it guides where to invest writing effort: transient notes are captured quickly; evergreen notes are crafted carefully [source: evergreen-notes.md].

## Composability

Because evergreen notes follow the [[atomic-notes-principle]], they are composable. They can be arranged into sequences (arguments), clusters (topics), and hierarchies (from specific to general). This composability gives linked note systems their power — the same building blocks can construct different structures depending on the question being asked [source: atomic-notes-principle.md].

## Relationship to the Zettelkasten

Evergreen notes draw heavily on Luhmann's [[zettelkasten]]. The key additions are the emphasis on declarative titles and the explicit acknowledgement that most notes people write are transient. Matuschak's framing helps practitioners decide which ideas deserve the deeper investment of evergreen treatment [source: evergreen-notes.md].
